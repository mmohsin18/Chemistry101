The Mole and Avogadro's Number

A mole is a unit used to count particles (atoms, molecules, etc.) and is defined as 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number).

  • 1 mole of carbon atoms = 6.022 × 10²³ C atoms = 12.011 g
  • 1 mole of textbooks = 6.022 × 10²³ textbooks (a stack 2.5 million light-years high!)

Formula Weight Calculations

Formula weight is the sum of atomic weights in a compound. Example calculations:

  • Sucrose (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁): 342.300 g/mol
  • Vanillin (C₈H₈O₃): 152.150 g/mol
  • Ammonia (NH₃): 17.0304 g/mol

Calculation for NH₃: 1 × 14.0067 (N) + 3 × 1.0079 (H) = 17.0304 g/mol

Stoichiometry and Mass Relationships

Stoichiometry converts between grams and moles using formula weights.

Example: How many moles of calcium phosphate (Ca₃(PO₄)₂) are in 50.0 g?

50.0 g ÷ 310.177 g/mol = 0.161 mol

Balancing Chemical Equations

Equations must balance atoms on both sides. Adjust coefficients only!

Example: Propane combustion:

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

Balancing tips: Start with elements appearing once, save H and O for last.

Limiting Reagents

The reactant that runs out first limits the reaction.

Example: 12.0 g C + 64.0 g O₂ → CO₂. Carbon is limiting (produces 1 mol CO₂ vs. 2 mol if O₂ were limiting).

Percent Yield

Actual yield vs. theoretical yield:

% Yield = (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) × 100%

Example: LP gas grill produces 27.3 kg CO₂ vs. expected 29.9 kg → 91.3% yield.

Key Concepts

  • Mole: 6.022 × 10²³ particles; connects atomic and macroscopic scales.
  • Stoichiometry: Mass-mole conversions using balanced equations.
  • Limiting Reagent: Determines maximum product possible.
  • Percent Yield: Measures reaction efficiency.
