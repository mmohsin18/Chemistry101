1. Dissolution of Ionic Salts

Reactions in aqueous solution are important components of physiology. When ionic salts are added to water, they either dissolve or don't dissolve.

Dissolution Process

If ionic compounds are to be dissolved, chemical bonds must be broken. Ionic bonds must be broken, and water molecules must separate (which requires energy). Then ion-water interactions form (which releases energy). This process is also called dissociation.

Dissolution of NaCl in water - water molecules surround and separate the ions

Examples

KCl(s) → K⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
AgNO₃(s) → Ag⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)

2. Types of Reactions in Aqueous Solutions

1. Precipitation Reaction

Appearance of a solid (precipitate) when two solutions are mixed.

AgNO₃ + KCl → AgCl(s) + KNO₃

2. Formation of a Gas

Production of gaseous products (effervescence).

HCl + NaHCO₃ → CO₂(g) + H₂O + NaCl(aq)

3. Acid-Base Neutralization

Reaction between an acid and a base to form water and a salt.

HCl + NaOH → NaCl(aq) + H₂O

4. Oxidation-Reduction (Redox)

Transfer of electrons between species.

8 H⁺ + 5 Fe²⁺ + MnO₄⁻ → 5 Fe³⁺ + Mn²⁺ + 4 H₂O

3. Solubility Rules

Category Compounds Exceptions
Always Soluble
  • Ammonium salts (NH₄⁺)
  • Lithium salts (Li⁺)
  • Sodium salts (Na⁺)
  • Potassium salts (K⁺)
  • Nitrate salts (NO₃⁻)
  • Acetate salts (CH₃COO⁻)
 None
Usually Soluble
  • Chloride, Bromide, Iodide salts
  • Sulfate salts (SO₄²⁻)
  • Ag⁺, Hg₂²⁺ (halides)
  • Pb²⁺ salts (cold water)
  • Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺, Pb²⁺, Hg²⁺ (sulfates)
Mostly Insoluble
  • Carbonates (CO₃²⁻)
  • Phosphates (PO₄³⁻)
  • Sulfides (S²⁻)
  • Hydroxides (OH⁻)
 Soluble with "always" cations or special cations (Ba²⁺, Ca²⁺, Ag⁺ for OH⁻)

Example: Is PbSO₄ soluble?

SO₄²⁻ is usually soluble but Pb²⁺ is an exception → Insoluble

4. Ionic Equations

Total Ionic Equation

Ag⁺ + NO₃⁻ + K⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl(s) + K⁺ + NO₃⁻

Net Ionic Equation

Ag⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl(s)

Identifying Spectator Ions

Spectator ions appear unchanged: K⁺ and NO₃⁻ in the example above.

5. Oxidation-Reduction (Redox)

  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons
  • Mnemonic: OIL RIG (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain)

Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

  1. Elemental state = 0
  2. Monatomic ion = ion charge
  3. Oxygen = -2 (peroxides: -1)
  4. Hydrogen = +1 (hydrides: -1)
  5. Sum = 0 (neutral) or ion charge

Example: Oxidation numbers in H₂O₂

H = +1, O = -1 → total = 0 → confirmed

8 H⁺ + 5 Fe²⁺ + MnO₄⁻ → 5 Fe³⁺ + Mn²⁺ + 4 H₂O

Half-reactions:

Oxidation: Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + e⁻

Reduction: MnO₄⁻ + 5e⁻ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O
