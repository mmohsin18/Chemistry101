6.1 Energy Fundamentals

Energy is the capacity to do work. Key forms include:

  • Radiant energy (from the sun)
  • Thermal energy (random motion of particles)
  • Chemical energy (stored in bonds)
  • Nuclear energy (in atomic nuclei)
  • Potential energy (due to position)
Key Concept: Heat is the transfer of thermal energy between systems at different temperatures.

6.2 Systems and Surroundings

Thermochemistry studies heat changes in chemical reactions by examining:

  • System: The reaction being studied
  • Surroundings: Everything else

System Types:

  • Open System: Exchanges both mass and energy
  • Closed System: Exchanges only energy
  • Isolated System: No exchange

6.3 First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted:

ΔE = q + w

  • ΔE = change in internal energy
  • q = heat exchange
  • w = work done
Example:

Problem: Gas expands from 1.6L to 5.4L against 3.7 atm pressure

Solution: w = -PΔV = -3.7 atm × 3.8 L = -1430 J

6.4 Enthalpy and Thermochemical Equations

Enthalpy (H) measures heat flow at constant pressure: ΔH = qₚ

Key thermochemical equation rules:

  • ΔH reverses sign when reaction is reversed
  • ΔH scales with reaction coefficients
  • Physical states must be specified

Endothermic: H₂O(s) → H₂O(l) ΔH = +6.01 kJ/mol

Exothermic: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) ΔH = -890.4 kJ/mol

6.5 Calorimetry

Techniques for measuring heat changes:

  • Constant-Volume Calorimetry: qᵣₓₙ = -(qᵥₐₜₑᵣ + qᵦₒₘb)
  • Constant-Pressure Calorimetry: qᵣₓₙ = -(qᵥₐₜₑᵣ + q꜀ₐₗ)
Example:

Problem: 869g iron cools from 94°C to 5°C (s = 0.444 J/g·°C)

Solution: q = m × s × ΔT = -34,000 J

6.6 Hess's Law

The enthalpy change of a reaction is the same regardless of the pathway taken.

Key Point: Enthalpy is a state function - depends only on initial and final states.

Application: Used to calculate ΔH for reactions that are difficult to measure directly
