Chapter 5: Gases

1. Introduction

Matter exists in three states: Solids, Liquids, and Gases.

Gases are the best understood due to minimal interactions.

Gases completely fill any container and exert pressure.

2. Composition of Air

  • 78.08% Nitrogen (N₂)
  • 20.95% Oxygen (O₂)
  • 0.93% Argon (Ar)
  • Water vapor (H₂O) varies up to 4%.

Liquid Nitrogen (N₂):

  • Boiling point: −196°C (−321°F).

3. Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT)

Gases behave ideally according to these six postulates:

  1. Gas molecules move in straight lines randomly at different speeds.
  2. Kinetic energy is proportional to temperature (Kelvin scale).
  3. Collisions are elastic—no energy loss.
  4. Gas molecules are tiny compared to the empty space between them.
  5. No intermolecular attractions exist between gas particles.
  6. Gas pressure results from collisions with container walls.

4. Gas Pressure

Measured in multiple units:

  • 1 atm = 760 mmHg = 760 torr
  • 29.92 inches Hg = 14.7 psi
  • 1.013 bar = 1013 mbar = 101,325 Pa

STP (Standard Temperature & Pressure):

  • 1 atm pressure at 0°C (273 K).

Pressure on Mt. Everest: 0.349 atm

Example Calculation:

If pressure in Missoula is 680 mmHg, convert to atm:

680 mmHg × (1 atm / 760 mmHg) = 0.895 atm

5. Gas Laws

Four fundamental gas laws:

Boyle's Law (Pressure-Volume Relationship)

P × V = constant (at constant temperature).

Formula: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

Example:

Chlorine gas at 946 mL and 726 mmHg is compressed to 154 mL.

New pressure:

P₂ = (P₁ × V₁) / V₂ = (726 × 946) / 154 = 4460 mmHg

Charles's Law (Temperature-Volume Relationship)

V/T = constant (at constant pressure).

Formula: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15

Example:

CO gas at 3.20 L and 125°C shrinks to 1.54 L.

New temperature:

T₂ = (V₂ × T₁) / V₁ = (1.54 × 398.15) / 3.20 = 192 K

Gay-Lussac's Law (Temperature-Pressure Relationship)

P/T = constant (at constant volume).

Formula: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Avogadro's Law (Volume-Moles Relationship)

V/n = constant (at constant temperature and pressure).

Formula: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂

Example:

Ammonia burns in oxygen to produce NO and H₂O.

How many volumes of NO from 1 volume of NH₃?

Answer: 1 volume NH₃ → 1 volume NO (at same T and P).

6. Ideal Gas Law

Combines all gas laws into a single equation:

PV = nRT

Where:

  • P = pressure (atm or torr)
  • V = volume (liters)
  • n = moles of gas
  • T = temperature (Kelvin)
  • R = universal gas constant:
    • 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K
    • 62.4 L·torr/mol·K

Example Calculation:

How many moles of air in 3.6 L lungs at 37°C and 1 atm?

n = PV/RT = (1 × 3.6)/(0.0821 × 310) = 0.14 mol

7. Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

Total pressure = sum of individual gas pressures in a mixture.

Ptotal = PN₂ + PO₂ + PAr

If air in Missoula is 680 torr, find P(N₂), P(O₂), P(Ar):

P(N₂) = 680 × 0.7808 = 531.0 torr

P(O₂) = 680 × 0.2095 = 142.5 torr

P(Ar) = 680 × 0.0093 = 6.3 torr

8. Real-Life Applications

1. Blood Gases:

O₂ and CO₂ pressures vary in lungs and tissues.

Example:

  • Arterial Blood: O₂ = 100 torr, CO₂ = 40 torr
  • Venous Blood: O₂ = 40 torr, CO₂ = 46 torr

2. Carbonated Beverages:

Pressure release causes CO₂ to escape (fizzing sound).

3. Hurricanes and Air Density:

Dry air is heavier than moist air due to molecular weight.

9. Problem-Solving Summary

1-State Problems: Given 3 variables, solve for the 4th.

2-State Problems: Compare before/after conditions.

Mass/Formula Weight: Use PV = (mRT) / MW.

Examples:

  1. Find MW of gas if 8.00 g occupies 22.4 L at 2 atm, 273 K.

    MW = 4.00 g/mol (Helium)

  2. Find volume of 2.50 moles O₂ at 25°C, 0.895 atm.

    V = 68.3 L

  3. Find pressure in an autoclave at 165°C if initially 1 atm at 100°C.

    P₂ = 1.17 atm
